UC Divestment Section 1H

Timeline and information...

The University of California controls one of the largest university investment portfolios with managing over $150 billion in assets. Because these investments consist of large stakes in industries such as fossil fuels, defense contracts, and global markets, UC’s financial decisions have long been the target of activism and pushback from their own students. We explore how UC divestment campaigns across decades unearth influences of neoliberal governance in UC administration, where financial decision making conflicts with demands from the campus communities which those funds are meant to serve.

What UC’s investment approach implies

  • Scale: roughly $150B in assets
  • Delegation: UC uses external/private managers for parts of the portfolio.
  • Benchmarks: Uses a tobacco and fossil fuel free index as a benchmark for performance of public equities (i.e., stocks), but includes defense and policing contractors.
  • Restrictions: tobacco is prohibited; country-based restriction noted for Sudan.
  • ESG framing: UC states ESG is incorporated as part of risk assessment, weighted with other “material” risk factors.

Big Picture Neoliberalism Analysis

Key Sources

  • LA Times
  • Left Voice

Neoliberalism transforms universities into market driven institutions focused on profit, surveillance, branding, and credentialing rather than institutions dedicated to critical inquiry and the betterment of the public. Financial incentives have led universities to shift from encouraging change through productive thought, to taking political stances based on what would be best for their public image. A good example would be the Board of Regents deciding to incorporate environmental sustainability, social responsibility and governance into their investment decisions, due to seeing them as risk factors over ethical commitments. This mindset is in direct opposition to student activists, who advocate for divestment from a moral standpoint, whether it be in opposition to war, climate change, or military occupation.

This dynamic is made clear when looking at the Neoliberal University pattern of response. During the Cornell Anti-Apartheid Movement, the Cornell Board of Trustees refused to meet any public demands, maintaining their position as around 1000 student protestors faced arrests. Eventually they made concessions by only partially divesting, still resisting the full demands of the students and maintaining a sizable chunk of their investment portfolio. It was a move that read more as a pacifying divestment while still maintaining their monetary interests rather than true change. When looking at the fossil fuel divestment movement, public demands were actually met in a “massive win”, with even an additional $1 billion invested in clean energy. However, the UCs were clear in that this was a financially driven decision, not a moral one. Furthermore, the 2024 Encampment movements protesting the Israeli militant occupation in Palestine were completely stonewalled, with Universities refusing to meet public demands. UC President Michael Drake even banned student organizations from further anti-Israel boycotts after federal pressure from the NSF and NIH threatened to withhold grants. This was a decisive decision firmly against the encampment movement, showing that they capitulated not to the student’s moral standpoints, but rather to financial pressure, showing the prioritization of monetary gain over morality.

Over the years, there is a clear pattern of profit being the priority of school boards since the inception of the Neoliberal University. As a result, critical thought and calls for changes in society are evaluated not by the moral arguments presented, but by the underlying market margins and overall risk assessment of supporting or opposing student movements. While institutions present themselves as spaces for critical inquiry and social progress, their response to said inquiry and calls for progress ultimately is governed by a desire to maintain financial stability and monetary growth.

Media portrayal of student divestment protests

The media is a powerful tool that can sway public opinion about all kinds of social and political issues. News outlets speak directly to the people and for the people in some cases. In the case of protests on UC campuses, the media has the power to influence the general public into thinking a certain way about the goals and response to the student protests.

The media can be a tool that allows people to create a story out of nothing, it allows issues that are unseen to be brought to public attention, but it can also be used to influence. Many news outlets are able to seem unbiased but no outlet can be entirely unbiased. The language can be unbiased in theory but the aspects they choose to focus on speak volumes on their agenda. For example, in the Reuters article, Trumps has begun to spin a narrative where students who are protesting against the genocide in Palestine are anti-semitic. The president has made claims that all students participating in these protests are actively participating against the rights of Jewish students and the university itself is turning a blind eye to the impacts the protests have and the feelings that may arise in some students when they see them. With the president's label as anti-semtic the protesters and their goal are lost in the media. Many people will fail to do more research and fail to recognize the protests' true goal is education and impact. It equally voices concern for the issue while also recognizing that there is true anti-semitism as well as Islamophobia emerging from this political debate.

The media can be outwardly biased with the intention of spreading narratives that allow the focus to be on their own story. In these biased articles, the articles focus on unimportant aspects of the protests to refocus the readers attention from the student cause. Leading one to believe that the university’s payment was an admission of guilt. UC Investment impacts

Key Sources

  • AFSCME
  • AFSCME
  • City on a Hill

Despite promises to support their community, UC investments are directly linked to entities that are responsible for contributing to housing insecurity amongst UC employees. Deshundre Richee, a father of two working as a custodian at UCLA Health, exemplifies the issue with UC’s predatory investments and simultaneous disregard for their workers. Though he provides a crucial service to one of the state's top healthcare facilities, Richee’s wages are not enough to secure him basic essentials. For the last two years, he’s lived at the Los Angeles Mission, a homeless shelter in downtown L.A.’s Skid Row neighborhood. His story is just one of the countless UC employees subject to housing insecurity and subpar living conditions. Rather than offer housing benefits the UC already provides for senior faculty and managers, UC continues to invest in companies that further the crisis.

A significant portion of UC employees face poverty and housing insecurity. In 2024, two-thirds of UC frontline service and patient care workers met U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development’s (HUD) definition of “low” or “very low” income. This reflects a failure of the UC system to provide their employees with living wages, but their investments in Blackstone’s Real Estate Income Trust (BREIT) only serve to further diminish quality of life for their employees. As the nation's largest corporate landlord, Blackstone is directly responsible for rising rent prices and the subsequent displacement of low-income families. The company also funneled over $14 million dollars into campaigns advocating against rent control measures in California. UC’s $4.5 billion dollar investment in BREIT is equivalent to almost 19,000 rental units.

Over the past few years, AFSCME Local 3299 has called on the UC to divest from Blackstone and instead focus funds back into the welfare of UC employees, citing the UC’s $4.5 billion dollar investment in BREIT as “a major driver of the housing affordability crisis” in California.

BlackRock, a former subsidiary of Blackstone, manages $2.1 billion dollars for UC, with $163 million invested in BlackRock itself. The company, headed by UCLA alumni Larry Fink, has invested $36 billion in military weapons companies. Fink himself has contributed massively to the New York City Police Foundation, which raises money to supply the NYPD with equipment and surveillance technology developed by the very companies with which his firm invests. The NYPD was deployed against pro-Palestinian protestors at Columbia University, engaging in the active suppression of student activism, and going as far as to physically assault protestors. This behavior is completely in line with the violent actions of officers at protests across the nation, including those at UCLA. Both the NYPD and LAPD engage in predictive policing methods reliant on facial recognition technology, software that LAPD officers claimed could be used in the investigation of the UCLA pro-Palestinian protests. The technology used to surveil students, employees, and faculty on UC campuses is both developed and bought with money passing through BlackRock. The cyclical nature of the UC’s suppression of speech and investment in security technologies is key to their ability to maintain control over their campuses and thereby ward off dissent. Recently, UC has come under major criticism for a cybersecurity mandate enforcing the strict surveillance of faculty research and communications. This mandate allows for data deemed “threat information” to be rapidly shared with federal agencies. Federal agencies, particularly ICE, pose a threat to student, faculty, and employee safety, leaving the entire community vulnerable to state violence.

Big Picture divestment analysis:

The "Big Picture" Neoliberal Analysis examines how universities have transitioned into market-driven institutions that prioritize financial stability, branding, and profit over moral accountability and critical inquiry. This framework suggests that student calls for social change are evaluated by administrations not on their ethical merits, but through the lens of underlying market margins and risk assessment.

Financial Risk over Ethical Commitment: The UC Board of Regents explicitly incorporates environmental sustainability and social responsibility (ESG) into investment decisions primarily as "material risk factors". This means social issues are weighed with the same gravity as other market risks, effectively treating human rights concerns as potential threats to the portfolio's performance rather than moral imperatives.

The Precedent of "Financial Wins": The UC’s 2020 fossil fuel divestment—while a landmark victory involving over $1 billion in assets—was framed by leadership strictly as a "financial risk" decision. This framing set a tactical precedent: if the only acceptable reason to divest is poor market returns, then movements demanding divestment on purely ethical or human rights grounds can be dismissed before the conversation even begins.

Selective Concessions and Pacification: During the 1980s Anti-Apartheid movements, institutions often resisted full divestment until pushed by extreme pressure. At Cornell, the Board of Trustees initially refused demands even as 1,000 students faced arrest. They eventually adopted "selective divestment," a move viewed by many as a way to pacify protesters while maintaining a sizable portion of their investment portfolio.

Prioritization of Federal and Corporate Funding: In the 2024 pro-Palestine encampments, the UC administration moved to ban student government boycotts following federal pressure from the NSF and NIH, which threatened to withhold grants. This demonstrates that the university’s stance is often dictated by the need to protect its primary revenue streams—such as federal research grants—over the moral standpoints of the student body.

Institutional Contradiction: The Big Picture Analysis highlights a fundamental tension: universities often invest in and profit from the very systems of oppression that they teach students to analyze and critique. In this neoliberal model, student calls for social change are not evaluated on their intellectual or ethical merits, but are instead processed through the lens of market margins and the overall risk assessment of supporting or opposing a movement. Consequently, the "Big Picture" of divestment reveals that for the modern university, the preservation of capital often supersedes the pursuit of justice.
