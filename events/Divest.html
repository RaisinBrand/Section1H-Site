UC Divest Coalition – Campaigns Today

Overview

Students across the UC, and especially UCLA, today have continued organizing for divestment through the UC Divest Coalition, with current active campaigns focused largely on divestment from Blackstone, the world’s largest “landlord” and private equity firm generally.

In recent years, students across the University of California system have organized campaigns calling on the university to divest from corporations connected to war profiteering, U.S. militarism, and displacement both globally and locally. Much of this organizing happens through the UC Divest Coalition, a network of student organizations founded in 2020 that works across several UC campuses, researching the university’s investment practices and demanding that the administration be more transparent about where UC money is going. While the coalition spans the entire UC system, it’s currently most active at UCLA, where student groups continue to direct campaigns toward the UC Board of Regents, the body that ultimately oversees the university’s investments.

The University of California controls one of the largest investment portfolios of any university in the world. UC investments manages roughly $150 billion across the university’s endowment, pension funds, and other financial pools. These funds are invested through a web of asset managers and private equity firms, allowing the university to generate returns through global financial markets. This structure makes it difficult for students, workers, and even many faculty members to see exactly where UC funds are invested. Because of this lack of transparency, many divestment campaigns have paired calls for divestment with demands for disclosure, arguing that the university community has a right to know how the institution’s financial resources are being used.

UC Divest organizers have done extensive research into the university’s investment portfolio, and their campaign materials and organizing timelines document repeated attempts by students to meet with the Board of Regents and UC administrators to discuss their findings and proposals—efforts which have largely gone nowhere. In many cases, Regents have simply declined to meet with student coalitions advocating for divestment or have not responded to requests at all.

With so many of the UC’s funds tied to companies connected to militarization, surveillance technology, and displacement, UC Divest identified several major investment targets in order to focus their campaigns. These include fossil fuel giant ExxonMobil, financial institutions BlackRock and Blackstone, and venture capital firm Sequoia Capital. These kinds of investments show how the modern university operates not only as a public educational institution but also as a major financial actor deeply embedded in global investment markets.

The Blackstone Campaign

Of UC Divest’s campaign focuses, one of the most active campaigns being waged today focuses on Blackstone, which is one of the largest private equity firms in the world. At UCLA, this has been led primarily by the Student Labor Advocacy Project (SLAP) and Students for Justice in Palestine (SJP), who frame the issue as both an international justice concern and a local economic one that directly affects students and campus workers.

Blackstone became a major target partly because of the scale of the University of California’s financial relationship with the firm, with billions of dollars invested across multiple Blackstone funds. Deeply involved in global real estate markets alongside many other markets (as it manages over $1 trillion in assets), the corporation is the largest corporate landlord in the world, a position that lends itself to an exploitative nature. Its business model involves acquiring large housing portfolios, raising rents, and extracting fees, practices that have severely worsened housing affordability in many regions.

The relationship between UC and Blackstone expanded significantly in 2023, when the university committed roughly $4.5 billion to BREIT while the fund was under heavy financial pressure. At the time, investors were attempting to withdraw large amounts of money, and Blackstone had begun restricting redemptions to slow the outflow. The UC’s huge investment essentially stabilized the fund during this period of near-failure. University officials said the investment could help support the creation of affordable housing, but organizers point out that rents in Blackstone-owned student housing tied to UC properties have continued to rise.

Organizers also argue that the investment makes little financial sense for the university itself. Blackstone’s BREIT (Blackstone Real Estate Income Trust) fund originally projected an 11.25% return on investment but has so far delivered closer to 1.3%. According to campaign researchers, reaching the originally promised returns would require massive rent increases across Blackstone’s housing portfolio in the coming years—enough to create a total return of +84% by December 2028—something that would deeply intensify displacement.

Despite all of these issues, today, the University of California has roughly $8–9 billion invested with Blackstone, making it one of the largest private equity relationships in UC’s portfolio. This raises intense contradictions, as while students and campus workers are dealing with an ongoing housing crisis, the university continues to invest heavily in—and in the case of BREIT in 2023, essentially bail out—a corporation that creates the very rent hikes and displacement affecting them.

Because of this, the Blackstone campaign has drawn support beyond student organizations. AFSCME Local 3299, the union representing tens of thousands of UC service and patient-care workers, has publicly called on the university to divest billions of dollars from Blackstone, with union leaders arguing along similar lines of it being unacceptable for the university to invest heavily in a corporate landlord while many UC workers cannot afford housing near the campuses where they work. This collaboration between student organizers and labor unions has helped broaden the campaign, linking university investment decisions to issues of housing justice, labor conditions, and economic inequality.

Alongside calls for divestment, organizers have also emphasized reinvestment, arguing that funds currently tied up in private equity and speculative real estate could instead support affordable student housing, basic needs programs, and other initiatives that directly benefit the university community. These demands have taken on greater urgency as students and workers across the UC system face rising housing costs, increasing food insecurity, and cuts to budgets for student programs and services.

Despite sustained organizing and growing public attention, UC leadership has resisted calls for divestment. University officials argue that investment decisions are guided primarily by fiduciary obligations and long-term financial considerations. Critics contend that this framing overlooks the ethical and social implications of how university funds are invested. The refusal of the Regents to meaningfully engage with divestment demands reflects a broader tension within the modern university: although institutions like the University of California present themselves as public-serving educational institutions, their financial practices increasingly resemble those of large investment funds operating within global markets.

The campaigns to divest from Blackstone and other financial institutions therefore raise broader questions about how public universities operate in an era of financialization. By challenging the university’s investment practices and calling for greater transparency and accountability, UC Divest organizers argue that the financial power of a public university should be used to support the well-being of students, workers, and surrounding communities rather than reinforcing the structures of global finance that contribute to war, genocide, displacement, and the general growth of imperialism.

For students, workers, and community members interested in learning more or getting involved, the UC Divest Coalition regularly shares organizing updates, research, and opportunities to participate through its Instagram page. The page includes a form for those who want to connect with one of the student organizations involved in the coalition or join ongoing campaigns. If the issues discussed here resonate with you, consider checking out the page and getting involved in the ongoing campaign for divestment and across the UC system.
