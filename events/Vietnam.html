Vietnam Divestment

Overview

Summary: The Vietnam War had a drastic fiscal toll that began to cannibalize the funds available to support.

The Vietnam War and the Rise of Campus Protest

The Vietnam War (1955–1975) emerged out of Cold War tensions and the broader U.S. strategy of containment aimed at preventing the spread of communism in Southeast Asia. Following the 1954 Geneva Accords, Vietnam was divided into North and South, with the United States backing the anti-communist South Vietnamese regime. What began as advisory support escalated dramatically after the 1964 Gulf of Tonkin incident, leading to sustained bombing campaigns and the deployment of hundreds of thousands of U.S. troops. By the late 1960s, the war had become one of the most controversial military engagements in U.S. history.

Economically, the war coincided with the tail end of the post–World War II economic boom. The U.S. entered the Vietnam era with relatively strong industrial output and global dominance, but wartime spending began to strain federal budgets. Military expenditures rose sharply without corresponding tax increases, contributing to inflationary pressures that later intensified in the 1970s. As David McNally argues, the war accelerated contradictions within U.S. capitalism, particularly the tension between social spending and military expansion.

One of the most visible symbols of corporate complicity in the war was the role of the Dow Chemical Company. Dow produced napalm, a highly flammable incendiary weapon used extensively by U.S. forces in Vietnam. Images and reports of napalm’s devastating effects on civilians intensified public outrage. Because Dow was a civilian corporation operating on U.S. soil, its role blurred the line between military policy and private enterprise.

University campuses became key sites of protest, especially when it became known that universities, including institutions within the University of California system, held financial investments in defense contractors like Dow. Students increasingly argued that their tuition dollars and university endowments were indirectly funding the war. This critique marked an important shift: opposition was not only moral or humanitarian but also structural, targeting the economic networks sustaining militarism.

By the late 1960s, student activism had become one of the most powerful components of the antiwar movement. Organizations such as Students for a Democratic Society (SDS) mobilized campuses nationwide. Protests escalated from teach-ins and rallies to building occupations and direct action aimed at military recruiters and corporate representatives.

Between 1967 and 1969, students across the country launched coordinated campaigns to pressure universities to sever ties with Dow Chemical. When Dow recruiters attempted to conduct interviews on campuses, students organized sit-ins and blockades, sometimes leading to violent clashes with police. These protests highlighted a growing understanding among students that the Vietnam War was not simply a foreign policy mistake but was deeply embedded in domestic political and economic structures.

The antiwar movement intersected with broader struggles of the era, including the Civil Rights Movement, feminist organizing, and labor activism, creating a multi-layered coalition challenging U.S. state power. Universities became central battlegrounds because they symbolized both intellectual authority and institutional complicity. As a result, campus protests in the late 1960s and early 1970s represented not only opposition to the war itself but also a broader critique of corporate capitalism, imperialism, and state violence.

Vietnam and Neoliberalism

Quick Summary: This section explores how the political and economic background of the Vietnam War led to the emergence of neoliberal policies. Additionally, it discusses the rise of neo-liberal militarism and debt control across the Global South.

As growing media attention and mass protests broke out, the tragedies of the Vietnam War were being broadcast across the nation. While support for military involvement declined, the U.S. had to find new ways to maintain global power. Historian David McNally discusses this shift in The Marines Have Landed: War and Imperialism in the Age of Globalization. He states, “America now found itself suffering from what came to be known as ‘Vietnam syndrome,’ hesitant to commit massive ground troops to fight guerrilla armies in the Third World. In the meantime, however, it had discovered a new weapon with which to discipline the South: debt.” Rather than facing public opposition to deploying troops under the justification of “fighting communism,” policymakers turned to economic pressure to force obedience in favor of their imperialist growth. The Vietnam War marks an important shift in history, as it portrays a turning point in neoliberal policies coming to the forefront.

The U.S. spent massive amounts of money during the Vietnam War, approximately 168 billion dollars. Due to the large amount of American dollars circulating, the gold exchange standard was failing as the U.S. did not have the gold to back the convertibility rate. This led to the removal of the Bretton Woods System in 1971 under President Nixon. Under this removal, global financial institutions and private banks were flooded with a surplus of U.S. dollars to lend. The Global South was eager to take these loans, as they were focused on developing their countries. But, as we’ve seen throughout the decades, these loans often came with conditions.

In 1979, Paul Volcker was appointed Chairman of the Federal Reserve. In an attempt to curb inflation, Volcker raised interest rates significantly. As McNally explains in the Global Slump, “Then came the Volcker Shock. As interest rates soared, one government after another in the Global South could not meet their payments.” Interest rates became so high that countries were forced to borrow more in order to meet payment demands. However, loans were leveraged at the price of austerity, forcing countries to cut back on social welfare spending and privatize resources. According to McNally, “In the span of a decade, from 1971 to 1980, Third World external debt grew eight-fold - from $70 billion to $560 billion.” However, debt did not replace the hidden fist; it worked alongside the military and private corporations to further the interests of capital at the expense of human rights.

The emergence of neo-liberal militarism post Vietnam War extended into universities through their investments in corporations aiding in war crimes. Student-led divestment protests erupted in the 70s in massive opposition against the Dow Chemical company and its production of Napalm. As neoliberal policies dominated the global economy, students across the globe have continued to fight for divestment and human rights. While the battle is not always won, students showcase a robust determination to challenge neoliberal policies that value capital over humanity.
